Humor generation is a useful way to study the limits of current language-model post-training. In domains such as mathematics or code, a generated answer can often be checked by an external procedure: a solution is either equal to the correct answer, or a program either passes the tests or fails them. Humor does not have that structure. A joke may be fluent, on-topic, and grammatically correct while still missing the intended effect. At the same time, the problem is not simply that humor is subjective. A prompt can admit many different good jokes, and the observed dataset usually contains only a small subset of them. For this reason, humor is not only a hard generation task, it is also a useful case for studying reasoning and reinforcement learning in non-verifiable domains \citep{Loakman2025,lemmens2026computational}.

This difficulty has become more important since the arrival of large language models. Earlier computational humor systems were narrow but interpretable: they relied on templates, lexical resources, or specific punning mechanisms \citep{ritchie2001current,amin2020survey}. Modern language models have much broader coverage, yet recent work shows that fluent text is not the same as strong humor competence. Large evaluations still place model systems below strong human performance, and recent studies on puns and joke understanding suggest that models often reproduce the surface form of humor more reliably than they capture its underlying mechanism \citep{Zhang2024HumorAI,xu2024good,cocchieri2025you,loakman2025comparing}. In other words, humor exposes a familiar weakness of contemporary models: they are good at continuing likely text, but weaker at open-ended search over many plausible and surprising continuations.

One response to this weakness has been to make the reasoning process explicit. Recent humor-generation systems increasingly rely on staged prompting, creative search, or theory-guided decomposition instead of one-shot completion. These approaches are motivated by a simple observation: good jokes are often not produced by selecting the first likely continuation, but by exploring associations, reframings, contrasts, and punchline formats \citep{chen2023prompt,zhong2024let,Tikhonov2024,Kim2025,ZhangJ2025}. The results are encouraging, but they come with a limitation. In most such systems, the reasoning structure is prescribed by hand: the researcher decides how many stages there are, what intermediate representations should be produced, and in what order the model should proceed.

A parallel line of work in post-training suggests a different possibility. In verifiable domains, reinforcement learning has recently been used to elicit extended reasoning behavior directly, rather than only through manual prompting or fixed pipelines \citep{Lightman2023,DeepSeek2025}. Verifier-free methods such as VeriFree and RLPR further suggest that some of this logic may extend beyond mathematics and code, replacing an external verifier with intrinsic probability-based objectives \citep{Zhou2025,Yu2025}. For humor, however, these formulations face an immediate mismatch with the task itself. They are built around a single-reference assumption, whereas humor is naturally multi-reference: for a prompt such as ``write a joke about a frog'' there are many acceptable outputs, and any dataset records only a few.

This thesis is motivated by that mismatch. Its central claim is that humor generation should be formulated not as exact imitation of an observed joke, but as probability allocation over a set of acceptable jokes conditioned on a prompt. That shift matters both theoretically and practically. Theoretically, it changes how verifier-free reinforcement learning must be defined in a creative domain. Practically, it changes what the dataset must look like: instead of isolated prompt-answer pairs, training requires prompt-conditioned reference sets with multiple plausible jokes attached to the same constraint.

The work therefore has two connected parts. The first is methodological and theoretical: it formulates a multi-reference extension of verifier-free reinforcement learning for humor generation and analyzes the consequences of incomplete observed reference sets. The second is implementational: it builds the data pipeline needed to make such an objective operational. In the current repository, this pipeline already covers corpus collection, dense embedding generation, keyword extraction, prompt construction, reference retrieval, candidate generation, and automatic evaluation. The missing stage is the training loop itself. Accordingly, this thesis should be read as a preliminary study in the exact sense intended by that term: it establishes the formal objective and the software required for the later training experiment.

The data source is a combined corpus of approximately 664k jokes assembled from the Short Jokes and rJokes collections. The full corpus serves as the retrieval pool, while a its keywords are sufficient to construct the multi-reference training dataset. The target experimental setting is narrow and controlled: Qwen3-1.7B and Qwen3-4B models in instruct and thinking modes will be compared against an MRVF-trained thinking variant, with training planned on NVIDIA H200 GPUs using a patched \texttt{trl} and \texttt{vLLM} for rollout and baseline inference.
