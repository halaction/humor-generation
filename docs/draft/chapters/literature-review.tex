
\section{Computational humor as a research problem}

Humor has long been treated as a hard case for natural language processing because successful jokes depend on more than grammatical well-formedness. They also depend on incongruity, shared knowledge, timing, and audience expectations. Early reviews already described humor as one of the more complex targets for artificial intelligence, noting that implemented systems were largely confined to narrow punning mechanisms and that broader humor processing would require substantial world knowledge and reasoning \citep{ritchie2001current}. Two later surveys reach a similar conclusion from different angles. \citet{amin2020survey} show that computational humor generation has historically traded breadth for control: template- and rule-based systems could work well with identifiable humorous mechanisms, but only within highly restricted domains. More recently, \citet{Loakman2025} and \citet{lemmens2026computational} argue that the large-language-model era has broadened coverage while leaving the central problem unresolved, since fluent text is not equivalent to humor that readers actually find funny.

A further difficulty is theoretical pluralism. Humor theory offers several recurring explanatory families---most notably incongruity-based accounts, but also superiority and relief traditions---yet no single framework exhausts the phenomenon. This matters computationally because different joke types rely on different mechanisms. \citet{kao2016computational}, for instance, formalize incongruity in puns as ambiguity plus distinctiveness and show that these variables predict human funniness judgments. Their work is important not only because it finds an application for a classical humor theory, but also because it illustrates the limits of narrow formalization: a model that explains homophonic puns does not automatically explain narrative jokes, topical jokes, or socially situated humor. Recent theory-driven work in humor detection starts from the same premise, namely that no single comprehensive humor theory is sufficient and that computational models typically learn only fragments of humor \citep{demarez2024thinc}. For the present thesis, the consequence is methodological: humor should be treated as a creative task with a multitude of potential mechanisms rather than as a single scalar attribute of communication.

\section{Evolution of approaches to humor generation}

Before the rise of large language models, humor generation systems were usually narrow by design. As summarized by \citet{amin2020survey}, earlier work often relied on hand-written templates, lexical resources, phonetic transformations, or tightly constrained generation procedures. These systems had an important advantage: they encoded at least one explicit humorous mechanism. Their disadvantage was low adaptability. They produced humor only under carefully chosen linguistic conditions and therefore did not scale well beyond punning, stock joke forms, or limited domains.

Large language models invert that trade-off. They can produce open-ended humorous text over a far broader range of prompts, but fluency does not guarantee robust humor competence. This is visible in recent large-scale evaluations. \citet{Zhang2024HumorAI} introduce a multimodal cartoon-captioning benchmark built from more than 250 million human ratings over more than 2.2 million captions, and show that current fine-tuning methods remain limited on creative tasks. Even strong systems such as GPT-4 and Claude remain below top human contestants. Humor-specific understanding studies point in the same direction. \citet{xu2024good} show that large language models exhibit what they call lazy pun generation: instead of fusing two meanings into a single compact pun, the model frequently states both related words or meanings separately, which satisfies the task superficially. This finding is important methodologically, because it shows that simple lexical-success metrics can overestimate pun ability. Evaluation must also distinguish structural quality and originality from mechanically assembled or copied wordplay. \citet{cocchieri2025you} construct a contamination-resistant humor benchmark and find that most language models still underperform humans on pun comprehension, resolution, and generation. Going beyond puns, \citet{loakman2025comparing} show that current models, including reasoning models, do not reliably explain all joke types, which highlights how much of the existing literature remains focused on relatively simple joke forms.

Taken together, these studies suggest that current models are better at reproducing the surface form of familiar jokes than at generalizing across humor mechanisms. This distinction is important for the present thesis. A system can be good at producing joke-like text without being good at humor in the stronger sense required by open-ended generation.

\section{The issues of humor evaluation}

Evaluation is a central difficulty in computational humor. Humor is subjective, but the problem is not only subjectivity in the colloquial sense. What counts as a good joke depends on audience, context, delivery, and the dimension being evaluated. Even dataset papers emphasize that humor varies across topic, time, and recipient, making it difficult to identify a single target variable \citep{weller2020rjokes}. For this reason, recent humor work increasingly avoids treating funniness as a scalar metric.

The clearest example is \citet{Sakabe2025}, who evaluate Oogiri responses on six dimensions: novelty, clarity, relevance, intelligence, empathy, and overall funniness. Their results show that current language models generate responses between low- and mid-tier human performance, but they notably lag in empathy. The authors further argue that this deficit helps explain why model-based evaluation does not reproduce human humor assessment well. The significance of this result is twofold. First, it shows that humor quality is not reducible to ``is this funny?'' Second, it shows that human and model judgments may privilege different qualities.

A complementary perspective comes from \citet{winters2025evaluating}, who compare human comedians and GPT-4 in a live improvisational setting rather than in an offline text-only benchmark. Their study is valuable because it isolates a stronger comparison point: both humans and the model must respond rapidly to the same audience suggestions. Human jokes are still preferred slightly more often, but the margin is not overwhelming, and the design highlights how much delivery and situational context affect humor evaluation. This is a useful corrective to benchmark-only thinking. A text that performs well in static judging is not necessarily the text that works best in real comedic interaction.

These findings also clarify the role of LLM-as-a-judge methods. Structured evaluation frameworks such as \citet{Hashemi2024} are useful because they replace one-dimensional scoring with explicit rubrics and calibration. However, that paper also shows that raw LLM rubric judgments do not automatically align well with human ratings. For humor generation, this implies that LLM judges are best treated as measurement instruments with a limited domain of validity, not as transparent replacements for human preference.

\section{The promise of reasoning for humor generation}

One of the most important shifts in recent humor-generation research is the move from one-shot continuation to explicit reasoning or staged creative search. At the prompting level, \citet{chen2023prompt} model humor generation through step-by-step comedy-writing instructions for GPT-3. \citet{zhong2024let} go further by arguing that standard chain-of-thought prompting is poorly matched to creative tasks, and propose a Leap-of-Thought paradigm for Oogiri-style humor generation that emphasizes associative jumps rather than strictly sequential decomposition.

More explicit generation systems strengthen this line of work. \citet{Tikhonov2024} reconstruct one-liner joke writing as a multistep process and report improvements over zero-shot GPT-4 and other baselines in human evaluation. \citet{Kim2025} frame humor generation as the combination of cognitive, social, and creative skills. In their meme-captioning study, skill-augmented systems substantially outperform large language model baselines and approach top-rated human captions for the target audience. In multimodal settings, \citet{ZhangJ2025} propose a theory-guided multi-stage reasoning framework intended to improve both interpretability and humor quality.

These papers are important because they collectively reject the assumption that humor is best produced by an instant completion. Instead, they model humor generation as a search process over premises, associations, reframings, and audience-sensitive choices, similar to how human experts approach comedy writing or any creative tasks. An informational gap between the premise and the joke which is only filled out during inference that may be latent. At the same time, they share a limitation that is directly relevant to the present thesis: the structure of the reasoning process is defined rigidly. The number of stages, the type of intermediate representations, and the order of operations are fixed by the researcher. Such systems are therefore strong demonstrations of the usefulness of reasoning, but weaker evidence that a model can learn for itself when and how to reason for humor.

\section{Standard post-training is a poor fit for humor}

The most straightforward route to improving humor generation is supervised fine-tuning on large joke corpora. In practice, however, that objective is a poor match for the structure of the task. Joke datasets contain many repeated setups, familiar wordplay patterns, and conventional punchline forms. As a result, supervised fine-tuning can improve stylistic plausibility without necessarily improving creative generalization. Large humor resources such as rJokes are valuable for scale and topical variety, but they were not constructed as multi-reference generation datasets in which many distinct outputs can simultaneously be correct \citep{weller2020rjokes}.

Recent literature strengthens this concern from two directions. First, humor-specific work shows that even very large preference datasets do not make creative alignment easy. \citet{Zhang2024HumorAI} explicitly highlight the limitations of RLHF- and DPO-style fine-tuning on creative captioning. Second, post-training work outside humor makes the memorization-generalization distinction sharper. \citet{Chu2025} show that reinforcement learning generalizes more effectively than supervised fine-tuning to unseen textual and visual variants, whereas supervised fine-tuning tends to memorize training patterns and struggle out of distribution. This contrast is especially consequential in humor generation, where a weak prompt can have many good continuations and where memorizing frequent joke shells is not the same as producing an apt and surprising punchline.

Preference optimization has its own difficulties. RLHF assumes that a reward model can learn a stable proxy for the target behavior, but humor judgments are noisy and heterogeneous. \citet{Gao2023} show more generally that aggressively optimizing an imperfect reward model can harm true performance. Humor-specific evaluation suggests that the risk is amplified in creative domains: \citet{Sakabe2025} show systematic divergence between human and model humor judgments, while \citet{winters2025evaluating} show that humor quality depends on aspects of performance that are hard to capture in offline textual reward signals. For these reasons, neither supervised fine-tuning nor standard preference optimization cleanly solves the central problem.

\section{Reasoning-oriented post-training}

The recent success of reasoning-oriented post-training has come mainly from domains with objective verification. \citet{Wei2022} show that intermediate reasoning can substantially change model behavior on tasks that reward multi-step inference. \citet{Lightman2023} extend this logic from prompting to supervision, showing that process supervision outperforms outcome-only supervision on the MATH benchmark. \citet{DeepSeek2025} then demonstrate that reinforcement learning can elicit long-form reasoning without relying on human-authored chains of thought, and the clearest gains are reported in verifiable domains such as mathematics, coding, and related STEM tasks.

This creates the central transfer problem for humor. Humor may also benefit from extended reasoning, yet there is usually no deterministic verifier that can declare a joke correct. Recent verifier-free approaches aimed at domains outside of mathematics and coding attempt to close this gap by replacing the explicit verifiable reward with intrinsic probabilities assigned to reference answers. \citet{Zhou2025} propose VeriFree and show that, under a single-reference assumption, maximizing the conditional probability of the reference answer given the prompt and reasoning trace is equivalent to maximizing an exact-match verifier objective in expectation. \citet{Yu2025} develop a related approach named RLPR where reward is formulated as a mean likelihood over individual tokens to enhance variance stability.

For humor, however, the single-reference assumption is not a minor technical detail, it is the wrong data model. Prompts such as ``write a joke about a frog'' inherently admit many plausible responses, and any dataset will observe only a small and biased subset of them. In other words, humor is both multi-reference and only partially observed. This means that verifier-free reasoning methods are conceptually relevant to humor generation, but not directly applicable without reformulating the reward around sets of references rather than single targets.

\section{Research gap}

The literature reviewed above leaves a precise gap. Humor-generation studies increasingly show that staged reasoning, skill decomposition, or theory-guided generation can improve outputs, but these systems mostly rely on rigid inference pipelines \citep{chen2023prompt,zhong2024let,Tikhonov2024,Kim2025,ZhangJ2025}. Work on reasoning-oriented reinforcement learning shows that useful reasoning behaviors can be elicited, but the strongest existing formulations assume either objective verifiability or a single reference answer \citep{Lightman2023,DeepSeek2025,Zhou2025,Yu2025}. Meanwhile, humor evaluation research shows that funniness is multidimensional, context-sensitive, and only imperfectly captured by model judges \citep{Zhang2024HumorAI,Sakabe2025,winters2025evaluating,Hashemi2024}.

The present thesis is positioned at the intersection of these findings. It claims that humor benefits from reasoning, but rejects the need to formulate a fixed creative script. It also expands on verifier-free reinforcement-learning, but argues that creative tasks require a multi-reference formulation because multiple outputs can be simultaneously good and the observed reference set is incomplete. The resulting research question is therefore whether verifier-free reasoning objectives can be adapted to partially observed multi-reference humor generation in a way that encourages creative search without collapsing into memorized training samples.
